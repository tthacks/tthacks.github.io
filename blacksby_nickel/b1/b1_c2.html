Chapter 2

Master Reylin was one of the trialmasters entrusted by the great families to train them for the trials. Reylin himself had trained Blacksby and his father, and his hair was getting grey, but Blacksby had learned the hard way not to underestimate Reylin’s skill. The man was graceful and cunning with a sword, and nothing got past him. He was a very serious man, but Blacksby had developed a deep respect and kinship with his master, and sometimes thought of Reylin like he thought of his father. He’d been training with him since he was a young boy.

Reylin arrived like he always did at the Nickel family’s estate, but he was accompanied by a small caravan. The trials did not take place in the valley of Wolfordshire, or any of the valleys of Man, but in the world beyond. They had a long journey ahead of them, but at least they would be traveling in relative comfort.

When Blacksby stepped out of his room, dragging his trunk behind him, Reylin was already standing in the courtyard, eyes trained on the blue sky with its puffy clouds. It was a good day to travel, although the weather was not guaranteed to be the same outside of the land of humans.

“Good morning, young master,” Reylin said, when he saw Blacksby. The young boy winced; he had never heard Reylin call him by his name, not even when he was very young. It was a certain personal touch he was going to miss on their long journey. Blacksby handed his trunk off to one of the servants loading supplies into the caravan and turned to face his parents.

His mother waited for him by their front door. While it had been his father to carry on the Nickel name, his mother had once been a fearless traveler in the wilds, daring to wander into spirit lands. When several children were taken from their village by trickster fairies, Elena Nickel had tracked them down herself and freed them. She was caught by the head of the trickster fairies, a devilish spirit named Hahao the Hunter, who had cursed her wandering spirit and forbidden her to use her legs ever again. That had been when Blacksby was very young, and he couldn’t remember his mother ever being a true wanderer.

“This trip will be the farthest you’ve ever been from home,” she said, “But you carry everything you need in your heart.”

These words, which Blacksby had heard so many times, finally felt like they meant something to him now. He gave her a hug in goodbye.

His father stood by, and when Blacksby and his mother separated, he tried to stand up tall and look proud. Blacksby’s appearance favored his father, although he had his mother’s dark, silky hair and fine nose. Arturus Nickel put a hand on his son’s shoulder.

“I’m proud of you, Blacksby,” he said, and hugged him as well.

It would have been a nice moment for the Nickel family, except the serene air was pierced by a high-pitched screech. Blacksby whipped around to find the source of the noise.

A woman was being pulled out of one of the caravan’s many canvas rooms, kicking and screeching. Her blonde curls tumbled everywhere as she tried to squirm out of the grasp of one of Reylin’s servants.

“Stowaway! What are you doing here?” the servant demanded, and then recognized her wild face — for how could anyone who’d worked in the Nickel household for any amount of time not recognize the face of Melie Agrippe? “Melie, is that really you?”

“Let go of me!” she said, and bit the hand of the servant—he dropped her instantly. But instead of running away, Melie planted herself firmly on the ground, arms crossed.

“Melie? What are you doing, stowing away on the caravan?” Blacksby demanded.

Her face turned beet red, hiding her freckles. “You’ve got all these people coming along with you. I don’t see why I can’t as well! You’re not going anywhere without me, Blacksby!”

Arturus sighed, and muttered something to his wife. “I knew there was going to be a tough time with the two of them,” he muttered.

Blacksby looked to Reylin, who was unflapped by the ruckus. He’d seen the girl around the Nickel household as well. Blacksby was waiting for his master to chide her, to dismiss her, or have her forcibly removed. But he didn’t. Reylin simply shook his head.

“It is customary for the young master to make this journey unaccompanied by his friends or family,” he said, “but customs never seemed to ever have any influence on you, young miss Agrippe. In that case, I don’t see how I have any power to ask you to leave.”

Melie looked like she was armed with a retort, but once she processed Master Reylin’s words, she realized that she’d already gotten her way. “What? Really?”

“We’ve got a long way to travel today, and I don’t want to waste too much of our daylight in human lands,” Reylin said, simply, “young master Nickel, young miss Agrippe, we are ready to depart as soon as you are.”
