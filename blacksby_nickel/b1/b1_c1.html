Chapter 1

Blacksby Nickel was from one of the eight great families, and as was his duty, the time had come to prove his merit.

The Trials of Eldersbrook were designed to either break you or remake you. Blacksby had been training for them his whole life. While other children his age played games in the square, he had been studying or in his family's courtyard, training with sword and staff. His only concession to a childhood was occasionally roaming the hills around Wolfordshire Valley with his best friend and neighbor, Melie Agrippe.

Years of training and exploring, as well as being raised by a well-off lord of the town, made Blacksby strong and well-disciplined. But out in the hills, wandering through thickets of heather and over babbling streams, Blacksby almost felt like he could be himself--or what he considered his genuine self to be.

But what did it matter who he thought he was? That was the purpose of the Trials--to teach you who you truly are inside. Even when he looked at Melie, with her golden hair like tangled rope and freckled cheeks, he knew that in another world, he might fall in love and grow old together, like so many in their beloved valley of Wolfordshire often did. But what about in this world, where he had a higher calling?

On the morning before his trials began, Melie and Blacksby climbed up to Witches' Rock to see the golden hills of Wolfordshire glow golden in mid-summer. The trip there had been quiet between them, and not just because of the difficulty. When they arrived at the top, Melie sat on the edge of the rock, looking not at her best friend but the sunrise before them. It was breathtaking, definitely, but she seemed on edge.

"I don't see why you're here," she said, "Shouldn't you be training?"

"Master Reylin said there isn't any need," Blacksby explained.

"What if you got injured on the climb up here? What if you couldn't participate in the Trials?" she continued.

A nerve twitched in Blacksby's forehead. "Is something the matter, Melie?" she didn’t normally worry and obsess like this.

Her nose twitched. "If you're leaving, I don't see why you don't just leave already. Why are you dragging it out?"

"I'm not dragging it out. These Trials happen in the autumn of my seventeenth year. Just like they have for the rest of the Nickel family, and the other seven great..." he trailed off, noticing that Melie was rolling her eyes.

"You think I don't know all this?" she spat, "You've mentioned it every day for as long as I known you. I just--I just forgot that you might actually have to go to these Trials and leave me!"

Melie patted her eyes dry on the sleeve of her flannel jacket.

Blacksby sighed at the sound of this. He may not have known himself well, but he knew Melie. And when she was upset, there was little to do to comfort her. After all, he had to leave. There was no use in pretending that her begging would change the order of the world.

"You're right. Maybe this was a bad idea," he said, feeling hurt, "I thought you'd want the chance to say goodbye."

He pulled himself up from the edge of the rock and turned around, to leave via the path they arrived, turning his back on the sunrise. Melie's cheeks were wet, and no amount of wiping her eyes was going to make it any better. He left to walk down the mountain alone.


Blacksby walked alone through the market, beginning to set up for the sunny summer weekend. Farmers and traders from the valley constructed tents and tables for displaying their wares. Not many people paid much attention to Blacksby, too absorbed in their own work. He had to keep reminding himself that it didn't matter, that nothing really mattered. It would be a while before he'd see his beloved valley again, but it was in his best interest--and theirs--to leave.

His parents seemed surprised to see him back so soon. His mother, looking tired (she always looked tired with those lines around her eyes), brightened up when she saw him.

"Come here, my precious boy," she said, holding out her arms from her wheelchair. Blacksby gladly returned the hug, breathing in the sweet smell of his mother's hair. His father, Arturus Nickel, was preparing breakfast for them in the kitchen.

"Already finished saying your goodbyes in town?" he asked. Blacksby looked away.

"They weren't as well received as I hoped," he sighed, "I need to finish packing. Master Reylin will be here in a couple of hours."

As he slinked off to his room in the houses' western wing, his parents exchanged a concerned look.

Blacksby had been prepared for this moment for weeks, his belongings were already packed. Instead, he spent the remaining hours before his master arrived in his room, reading the maps of the world. They were beautifully crafted relics, with fine pen and paint marking the gashes in the land where the valleys of Man nestled among the mountains and hills and plains of the spirit land. There were eight valleys of Man, just like there were eight great families. His, the Nickels, was the only one from Wolfordshire, easily the smallest valley of them all. Legend said that the valleys were carved by the great Titan spirit Gyar Ada himself, by digging his great dragon claws over the surface of the earth. Blacksby ran his fingers over each gash, overflowing with human towns and farms, and imagined what it would be like to be a great spirit, let alone a titan. He’d never seen the titan spirits himself, although he was familiar with their images; paintings of the various titans graced the sides of Wolfordshire’s library. Ool Olang, flying low with his great owl wings; the formidable bear shape of Yi Song; Kal Alli, the fish; Wei Ha, the frog. Blacksby thought for a moment of walking through town to see those paintings again, but he didn’t want to risk running into Melie again, who would likely just run off in tears.

He buried his face in his palms, ashamed for angering and embarrassing her. His only friend… he should have been more kind. Now he’d return from his trials a hero, and she’d never look at him again. Or worse, he’d never return, and she’d always remember him as a coward and a failure.
