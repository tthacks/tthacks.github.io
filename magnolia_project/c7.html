Chapter 7

Michael wasn’t there the next day of school. Or the next. I had to do my chemistry labs all by myself, which was liberating but lonely. He missed three days before I started to wonder what had happened to him. Was he sick? Out of town? Michael always seemed so happy to be at school. The monotony of a day never seemed to wear on him. I couldn’t think of a reason why he wouldn’t come—unless he was trying to avoid me.

On the fourth day of his absence, my worries reached a head. “Mrs. Blackburn?” I approached our chemistry teacher after class.

“Yes, Grace?” she asked, putting away the chemicals we used that day.

“I was just wondering if you were in touch with Michael. My lab partner,” I explained.

“No, he hasn’t sent me any notes. But he must have that awful stomach bug going around if he’s been out this long,” she replied, briskly. “Has he asked you to bring him his makeup work?”

“Makeup work?” That was when I realized my opportunity to meet with him again, “Yeah, he did. I can drive it over to his house this afternoon.”

“Fantastic,” she handed me a thick stack of papers from within her desk. I smiled, wished her a good afternoon, and slipped the papers in my backpack.

Michael’s house was on the far edge of town, next to the old rock quarry. I biked there right from school, enjoying the fall breeze as it rushed under the neck of my sweater. His house seemed normal enough. It was long and low, and land around it was thickly wooded. The only unusual thing about it was that the perimeter is bounded by a tall cedar fence on all sides. That, and the window shades were all pulled down low despite the beautiful weather.

I parked my bike in the driveway and rang the doorbell, my backpack still on my back. The door opened just enough for the eye of a woman to peer out at me.

“Hello?” she asked, almost wary. I could only see a sliver of her face. The rest was blocked by the chain still attached to the door, “What do you want?”

“Hi, my name is Grace,” I introduced myself, although it was hard to talk to this lady hiding behind the door, “I’m a friend of Michael’s, from school. Is he home?”

“Michael’s friend?” she seemed to think this over, as if Michael has never had a friend, and it’s an unknown concept, “Is he expecting you?”

“No, I just thought I would drop by. Give him some schoolwork he missed,” I held up the folder, “Can I see him?”

She frowned and shook her head. “No. I’m sorry. Michael is very sick.”

“Oh,” I couldn’t hide the disappointment in my voice. Confident that she wouldn’t let me past that little golden chain, I held out the folder. “Can you at least make sure he gets this?”

I pushed it through the crack in the door, and she accepted it.

“Thanks,” I said, because she wouldn’t. Then I stepped away from the door. She closed it, but I can’t shake the feeling that she was still watching me out of the peephole as I walked away from the house and back to my bike. Michael certainly came from interesting stock, I’ll give him that. As I walked away from the house, I heard the sound of chopping wood in the backyard. Rustling branches, dragged across the lawn.

I left my bike behind and walked to a corner in the fence, where the boards didn’t exactly line up. Michael was in the backyard, dragging a heavy tree branch across the ground to a pile. He certainly didn’t seem sick. In fact, he looked completely normal.

“Hey, Michael!” I yelled to him, “There you are!”

He turned to look around at the fence, and I grabbed the top to push myself up. This fence was taller than any I’d ever climbed, and the surface didn’t have any footholds. I finally pushed off the boards and made it over the top. The climb down is a little easier, but not by much. Michael watched me, his mouth agape.

“How did you find where I live?” he asked, alarmed.

“The school phone book. Mrs. Blackburn gave me your makeup work. You’re welcome, by the way,” I said. Michael’s eyes popped, shocked to discover that the school would give out his personal information so easily. “If you’ve been ill, you’re certainly looking healthy. I guess you’ll be coming back to school soon?” I landed on the ground with a thud.

“Stay away from me!” he brandished his hand axe, blade out, and started to back away. His hands shook, making the ax bob up and down as he looked at me like I was a wild animal.

“Michael, I just—”

He threw the axe to the ground and took off up the base of a maple tree like a spooked squirrel. Up he went, one branch after the next, until he was halfway up. I ran to the base of the tree to see where he was going.

“If you don’t leave now, I’ll—burn this tree to the ground! I swear, I’ll do it!” he pulled out something small from his pocket—a box of matches.

“Michael, that’s insane,” I demanded, “Is something wrong? Please, just tell me. Maybe I can help!”

He was balanced precariously between a fork in two limbs, fifty feet up. “I should have known not to trust you. Or anyone! Of course it has to be the one person I decide to trust in this whole town, ” he waves his arm around so I can see the matches in his hand, ready to strike against the box. “I bet you’ve been working for the Magnolia Project this whole time—just like your father was!”

“Magnolia Project?”

“Don’t pretend you don’t know. That paper in your father’s study—it made it all clear. I’m glad he’s in prison. I can’t believe I fell for it. I can’t believe I thought I was helping you,” Michael sneered.

“Take that back,” I said, creeping closer to the base of the trunk.

That was it. I grabbed the bottom of the trunk and started to climb. I didn’t have a plan, I just wanted to reach him and shove him out of this tree like he deserved. It was pretty steady Maple tree, very old and easy to climb. There was always a branch just within reach to pull me up. But the higher I went, the harder it got. Michael scuttled up onto a higher branch, but the branches were getting thinner and more flexible. He bounced and swung slightly. I tried not to look down, but the branches on the ground kept getting smaller and smaller.

At last, I got to a part of the tree where there were no more branches for me to grab. Michael was still out of my reach. When I looked down to search for another branch, it bent under my feet. The ground started to spin beneath me. My righteous anger faded to panic and I clung to the trunk to steady myself.

“What? Afraid of heights, traitor?” I hugged the trunk even tighter and shut my eyes. How high up am I? What if I can’t ever go down?

“I’m—fine,” I gritted my teeth, “You ran off from my house and I don’t hear from you in days? What about that makes me a traitor? I’ve been worried about you,” Above me, I could feel the tree shake and sway. Michael climbed down a little bit. He got to a branch close to mine and hung from it as if he was totally comfortable. I tried my very best not to look down or think about my fragile body’s numerous intact bones.

Afraid of falling is a better name that afraid of heights. After all, I can be in an airplane, or on a mountain, or even the top of a tall building with no problem. But the moment the ground stops being something I take for granted, my muscles stop working the way they should.

He looked down at the ground, “If you’re not here for that, then what do you want from me?”

“Friends don’t disappear off the face of the Earth,” I gritted my teeth, “and not tell anyone.”

Michael’s voice softened, realizing I just called him my friend for the first time. Although, in my current state, it might also be the last. “I’m sorry I vanished,” he explained, his pack of matches returning to his pocket, “It’s… a long story.”

“Does it have to do with the Magnolia Project?” I asked, and Michael stiffened up again, “I found that in my father’s study, but I’ve never heard of it before. Do you know what it is?”

Michael didn’t answer, just climbed to a closer branch so he can be more comfortable. He looked out at the leaves all around us, already starting to turn shades of red and yellow. Nature really knows how to go out with a bang.

He cleared his throat. “My mom freaked out when I stayed so long at the library. She doesn’t like it when I stay out late. She grounded me and said I wasn’t allowed to go to school.”

“But that was hardly late,” When I say this, Michael looked away. Staying out for an extra hour was hardly worth a punishment of four days in solitary confinement.

“Michael?” a voice called from outside the second-story window: his mother, a voice of worry. “Who are you talking to? Is there someone there?”

“No one’s here, mom. It’s just me,” he yelled back. Then turned to me. “You should probably go. If she finds out you broke into the backyard, then you’ll never see me at school again.”

I didn’t want to argue, just climbed slowly down the tree with the help of Michael’s directions. Before I knew it, I was back on solid ground.

“Thanks for picking up my makeup work,” he said.

“Are you coming back to school ever?” I craned my neck to see him through the trees.

“Probably Monday.” He jumped down to a lower branch, “her over-protective phases never last longer than a week.”

“Is there anything I can do to help?”

“Just promise you’ll never mention the words Magnolia Project to me or another soul ever again.”

I nodded. If it meant Michael can be my lab partner, and hell, even my friend, I’ll do that. But that didn’t mean I wasn’t going to stop looking into it. I needed to clear my father’s name, and this has only piqued my interest. After slipping through the fence and back to my bicycle, I gave the house another good look. It’s hard to do that between the boxwoods and the tall fence. I can only wonder from what Michael’s mother needs to protect him.
