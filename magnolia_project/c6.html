Chapter 6

Late at night, when my brothers were in bed and it was dark in my room, I gave up even trying to sleep. The way Michael dashed out of the house, as if he had seen a ghost in my father’s study... As a kid, I always imagined he did something good, something that was going to save the world. Probably because that’s what he told me he was doing. This morning I woke up, positive that my father was innocent. Now, going to bed, I wasn’t not so sure.

My father walked through my dreams, his old lab coat over his orange jumpsuit. I chased after him to ask about the fire, but he disappeared into a puff of smoke. There was a sound of a television behind me, a clunky old CRT, playing news footage of a fire. Michael sat in front of the television, his face illuminated by the screen as he watched it intently from an old, ratty chair. Behind him was a mahogany door, the one that led to my father’s study. It wasn’t a particularly scary dream, but a confusing one, and I woke up with a pounding headache from trying to make sense of it all. My alarm clock said 3:20 AM. I had to wake up again in 4 hours. But for some reason, I thought falling asleep again would be harder than waking up.

My feet slid out of the covers and hovered over the carpet before settling down. The house was silent, and a glass of water and possibly a sandwich would calm my aching head. I stood up, careful not to let the floorboards creak.

As I crept out towards the stairs in my socked feet, the door of my father's study caught my eye. It's not that it's different from any of the other doors in the house; mahogany, with a golden doorknob. It's just that my mind knew what was beyond the door.

Back when he worked as a scientist, my father used to stay holed up in his study for hours at a time, working on some important grant paper or thesis. He would request his meals in there, but my mother would drag him out at dinnertime and force him to eat with us. I would sneak him cookies or other treats from the kitchen between those hours though, hoping to get a peek at what he was doing. His study was on the second floor, at the end of the hall. After he started working at the Shop n’ Save, his study demanded less and less of his time. I could find him in there in late evenings, if the first floor got too drafty, reading a book or a dozen articles. He loved articles and sometimes spent the entire morning reading the newspaper cover to cover.

After Michael left, I could not settle the itching feeling I felt inside of me. Even though he didn’t work in there anymore, surely his old papers still lived there. Papers too important to leave at the library. My mother would joke that the study was just storage for all of his knick-knacks and threatened to transform it into a laundry room, but she never did.

My hand rested on the handle and I turned it slightly. It was unlocked.

I pulled the chain on the lamp on his desk, so that the subtle light illuminated the room and chases the shadows into the corners. The floor was covered with a soft pale green rug that stretched almost all of the way to the corners of the room. At the center was a cherry wood desk, framed by the green-curtained windows behind it. Behind it sat a luxurious brown leather chair. There were some stacks of papers piled on the desk with military precision, lying next to the lamp. Bookshelves lined the walls and held pictures of our family. In one of the older ones, my mother and father are standing together, looking radiant and young. I sit directly in front of them, smiling a toothy smile with two gaps on the sides where I had lost baby teeth. To my left is Trevor, always the smaller of the two. My messy blonde hair is done up in curly pigtails, a style I haven't worn since elementary school. We all look so happy; I can't help but smile along.

My father's meticulous personality extended to the organization of his desk, but the drawer is open and some of the papers have shifted from their original places, disturbing the dust. I begin to straighten everything back to where it was when I realize there's an envelope on top, separate from the rest.

The seal has already been roughly broken, so I flick open the flap of the envelope and pull out the letter. It's addressed to DR. TERRY HALL at our home address from a DR. HENRY JAMES CHARLES.

Terry,

I’ve returned early from the conference in London, and I’m writing this in a rush because my patience has reached its end.

You’re on the wrong side of this issue. I know you’re holding out on me. You still have the files I’m looking for. If you continue not to share crucial information, then you can consider yourself an enemy of progress. If it wasn’t enough for you to lose your job and respect from your peers, I’m not sure if you’ll listen to me, but I have to try. A young girl’s life is on the line. You’re not protecting anyone.

HJC

I read the letter over and over again, from top to finish. The date says it was written two days before the fire. Two days? Maybe Henry James wasn’t in London like I thought. His alibi was no good. Not to mention, there was nothing cordial about this letter at all. In fact, for long time friends, he seems downright hostile. Consider yourself an enemy of progress? And what was this about a young girl’s life being at risk? My heart started to race. Was this why my dad went to their old lab? Had he been meeting with his old lab partner? But then what—or who—caused that fire?

I lunged for the cabinet, fussing with the hinge until it let me in. I needed to know what my dad’s last project was, now more than ever. And if it was never published, then surely it was here. There were tons of folders, enough folders to organize an army—but every single one was empty. Even the labels were cleared out, their plastic covers bent as if my father did not do it carefully. Like he was in a rush to be rid of them. All the papers were gone, but the tabs remained. One of them in the back simply said, THE MAGNOLIA PROJECT.

I turned off the desk light and left my dad's study, closing the door on the way out. Then I climb back into bed and turn out my light, staring out into the darkness with hundreds of unanswered questions and no one to ask.

The letter sat on top of my bedside table.
