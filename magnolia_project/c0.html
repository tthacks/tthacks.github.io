Prologue

Despite the late hour, the city still glowed with life.

The Schmidts’ BMW cruised through the city streets. It was a beautiful new car. The suspension was smooth and the windows kept out the sounds of the world. Lights from the clubs on either side spilled out into the streets, and the neon signs added color to what would otherwise be sleeping black and white landscape. The lights reflected off the black body of the Beamer in slanted versions of themselves. The car was painted with the lights of the city.

Garland's father drove in the front seat, relaxed, his elbows resting on his lap and his fingers gently on the wheel. His mother sat beside him, in a new black dress. Everyone loved it at the party. They cooed over Garland too, in his collared shirt and tie, calling him Little Man and loading up his plate with cookies from the kitchen. Garland loved the attention. Important people didn’t give it out easily.

The Beamer pulled to a stop at the red light on High Street. "How's it going back there, little man?" His father asked, looking back in the rear view mirror at his son. Garland yawned and rested his head on the window to look outside, where the night stretched on. He watched as two women walked along the street, short feathery dresses bouncing along the boulevard. Dancers, probably, finally beginning their night as the last show came to an end.

The light turned green and his father hit the gas. Garland turned his head to watch the dancers walk past, but he saw the headlights of a red pinto flying towards him. It didn't stop at the line, didn't even see the red light. Its headlights consumed the window and collided with the car.

At the moment of impact, all Garland noticed was the bright light. Compared to the night, even the sleepless night on the boulevard, this light was blinding. He screwed up his eyes against it as he slid out of his seat.

When his eyes adjusted, he realized the light wasn't headlights. They were florescent lights, and one of them was flickering uselessly above him. Before he could sit up, a stranger grabbed his hand.

"Little boy, how did you get up here?" It was a woman in a dark green apron. Garland sat up to look around.

The car, the street, the night lights and the dancers--none of them were here. Instead of neon signs, there were cardboard ones hanging from the roof. ORANGES: 50%. INCREDIBLE PRICES. Instead of dancers, there were shoppers, pushing carts aimlessly through the aisles. Underneath him was a crate of melons. He was in the produce section of a dingy 24-hour market, in the cantaloupe display. A few melons rolled off to the side on the ground. A button on the woman's apron said SHOP AND SAVE SUPERMARKET. She helped him out of the crate of melons, already asking questions. "Where's your mother?"

His mother... And his father... The moment his feet touched the linoleum floor of the supermarket, he took off, breaking free of the woman's hand. The automatic doors swung open for him and he pelted down First Street, Second Street, Third Street...

There, at the corner of Third and High, sat his father's beamer. Its black exterior still reflected the lights of the city, but in a disjointed sort of way. Its smooth edges had become hard angles. A streak of red from the Pinto that assaulted it marked the passenger's side door. His parents were still inside.

"Mom! Dad!" He yelled from the curb, hoping they would look to him, smile, call him Little Man one more time. Curtis and Eliza Schmidt stayed still, their bodies unaware that their son was calling to them. Their son, who until a few seconds ago, had been in that car with them, cruising down the street on a Saturday night.

The cops came not long after, and a crowd gathered nervously around the intersection. They wanted to see what happened, but no one stood close enough to try and help. When the police arrived, they barricaded the streets. No one noticed the blonde boy in the collared shirt crouched along the curb, clutching his head as if his brains were about to spill out. No one came out of either car. A report drifted from one of the EMTs. "...two passengers in the BMW, one in the Pinto. Must have been a drunk driver. No one could have survived that crash."

Garland wanted to yell at them. I did! I survived the crash! I'm right here! But no one would believe him. He didn't have a single scratch. No EMT would believe a boy could evaporate from the car on impact. Garland crouched down on the curb and scrunched up his eyes until he saw fireworks, wishing he could have taken his parents with him when he disappeared into thin air.
