Chapter 4

It’s been a week, and the public interest in my father’s arrest has dropped off significantly. There’s still the occasional loser in school trying to get me to tell him if my father’s innocent or not. But the occasional loser I can deal with. Unfortunately, my quest for justice is not going so well. All I have on the blog is a blank page. Honestly, I’m not even sure where to start. I’m not a detective.

“Hey, Augustine!” a voice called to me across the school lawn. My blood boiled, picturing a jeering jock trying to get me to open up about my father, and I sped up my march, trying to escape to the bike racks before its too late.

“Leave me alone,” I marched away from Michael, towards the bike racks. About six bikes were piled on top of mine, so it’s not the speedy getaway I imagined. I wrenched handlebars left and right to dislodge my bike from the heap. In this time, Michael managed to catch up to me. Ever since I worked with him that one Chemistry class, I haven’t been able to shake him. Mostly because Kirk kept working with Christie, that girl in the yellow outfit. Unlike the others, Michael couldn’t take a hint. He kept asking me about my father.

“I’m not here to mock you. I’m really sorry about what happened to him,” he insisted, “If you’re trying to prove him innocent, I want to help you!”

“Help me? What sympathy do you have for my father? You’ve never even met him,” There was one green bike that simply will not let go of mine. Every time I shook it, it stayed stubbornly interlocked.

“I know what it’s like to be locked up against your will,” he said, “It sucks.”

“What? Did your parents send you to your room for playing with matches?”

“If I were you, Augustine, I wouldn’t be so hostile,” he narrowed his eyes, “Friends just want to help out friends when they’re in trouble.”

“Friends? What makes you think we’re friends?” I gave up on shaking my bike loose. Maybe if I stood them both up, I could pinpoint the problem. Sure enough, the pedal of the green bike was lodged in my spokes. Without waiting for me to ask for help, Michael rolled the pedal backwards and pulled it out. My bike was free.

“You’re in trouble. I’m trying to help you,” he shrugged and set the green bike down gently, so it won’t tangle with the others. “Ergo, I’m your friend. Or at least trying to be one.”

I couldn’t help but notice Kirk is nowhere to be found. Maybe he was on to something about friends being there for each other.

“Don’t they keep all public records at the library?” Michael was persistent. He grabbed his bike off an adjacent rack and buckled his helmet. His parents must be real sticklers for the rules, since no one over the age of twelve wore a bike helmet in this town. “We could find out who owned the building. Maybe they have someone who hates them. Someone with motive!”

I’m pretty darn stubborn. And I don’t like to admit when other people are right. But this is the first lead I’ve had all week.

“Alright, lead the way,” I sighed. Michael’s face lit up with a smile as bright as a neon sign.


The library was an old stone building at the center of campus, with whimsical gargoyles on the outside guarding the knowledge of the town. Golden letters read UNIVERSITY LIBRARY above the wide oak doors. The library had five floors, and when you stand in the atrium and look up, you can see all of them. Books lined every corner on every shelf. The air had that dusty, vanilla smell to it, as the glue holding the spines of old books together broke down. Michael makes a beeline towards the reference desk. I stare longingly at the fiction section as we pass it, wishing we had time to stop.

The librarian sitting at the desk had thick bottle cap glasses. He was busy sorting through a few thousand index cards when we approach him. He looked up at our arrival.

"I never forget a face, you know. Just give me a moment," he pointed at me with a scraggly finger, and then snapped when it comes to him. "Of course! You’re Adelaide’s daughter!" What little hair he had left on his spotty head was white and curly, but his wrinkles arranged themselves quite nicely into a smile.

"Um, hi," I nervously returned his grin, desperately trying to remember this man's name, or how he knows me. I didn't come to the library often, but when I did, I steered well clear of this section unless I'm doing a research project.

"Of course, or course, you probably don't remember an old fellow like me; I'm Dr. Percy Warbler. A friend of your mother’s," He explained, but then dropped his voice. “I heard about the incident… very unfortunate. How is she doing?"

"She's fine," I said, uncomfortable. As far as I know, I have never met this man before. He must be a billion years old. But this was where she worked, and she must have tons of friends around the campus. I kept smiling vaguely until Michael rescued me from the conversation. I didn’t want to talk about this issue with random strangers; I feel like I’ve been dodging questions left and right all day.

"Excuse me sir, do you know where the public records are?" Michael asked, and then Percy points off to the left, in the section behind him. A glass door closed it off.

"Just through there and down the stairs," he says, and Michael thanks him. Percy looked like he's about to strike up another conversation with me, so we make a show of leaving towards the room.

The door led to a set of dimly lit stairs into a small cellar beneath the library. A bare light bulb hung from the ceiling, and Michael pulled the chain, illuminating the room. Racks and shelves stretching as far as they can through the room are teeming with presentation-sized boards, bound books, and paper-clipped stacks. The whole arrangement is an organization nightmare; it's also so dusty, I can't imagine anyone's come in here in a few months. I choked back a sneeze, cover my mouth with my sleeve, and follow Michael down the rows.

"I don’t even know when to start. My father retired eight years ago, but who knows if they still have it? Or where they even are?" I commented.

Michael points to a small computer terminal, dusty just like everything else in this room. The technology looks like it was installed in the eighties and never replaced. Despite that, it looks like it works. The screen is blue with white writing: CEDAR VALLEY LIBRARY RESEARCH DATABASE. “I’ll check the Macintosh,” he says, and bends down to type something in.

While he searches, I wander the aisles. There’s an entire wall of Popular Science, a fort made of Encyclopedias. Despite the age of the technology, most of the publications seem pretty up-to-date, even though their labels are handwritten and becoming illegible.

"Check the third row, number 032.32," replies Michael as he clicks through some menus. “And 045.04… and 066.21.”

“Not so fast,” I warn him, and run down the aisles to find the different publications. “What about housing records? I want to know who owned the St. Clare Street building.”

“I think I saw that upstairs,” he says.

I pause in scanning titles. "Michael, where do you come from? Why did you move to Cedar Valley?"

He laughs, which really takes the mystery out of it. Everyone at school has been asking, wondering where he came from. And he thinks it’s such a trivial question? "I'm not from anywhere, Augustine. I've lived in Cedar Valley just about my whole life."

"Really? But I've never seen you before."

"I live on the far side of town."

"And you never went to school?"

"My mom home-schooled me."

"Why did you stop?"

“It’s kind of hard to play lacrosse on a team when you’re the only person there,” he says. It explains everything, I guess, but I’m not sure if I believe him on the lacrosse thing. They have plenty of recreational teams made up of homeschool students.

I continue perusing the racks with him in silence, scanning the shelves. I skip forward a few rows when I realize I'm amongst papers that only go back three years or so. Before long, I have a handful of my dad’s work, and Michael finds me an empty bin in which to carry all of it.

"Come on, this will be easier upstairs in better light," he marched towards the steps and I follow, pulling the light chord on the way out. The room returned to darkness.

We come out of the room and head straight to one of the study desks available, where Michael deposited the bin with a flurry of dust. I took out one of the magazines and flip through it until I find an article written by my dad.

We worked diligently as the sun in the window sank lower and lower. Even though I couldn’t understand half of the things on those papers, they don’t seem like the kind of things that cause controversy. It’s not like he worked with stem cells, or genetic modification, or climate change. It's the same every time; flip through the papers, scan the contents, check the date, close it back up, place it to the side. Michael flipped through the online housing record.

Occasionally, one of us would blurt out something that sounds interesting or important. Michael piped up. “When your father was paying rent on the lab, he wasn’t the only one. He cosigned it with someone named H.J. Charles.”

“He was my dad’s lab partner,” I explained, “Real eccentric dude. He still comes over for dinner sometimes.”

“Any feud he had with your father? Maybe one of them took credit for something the other did.”

“I can’t imagine that happening. They’re best friends. Besides, Henry James isn’t even in the country right now. He’s on vacation. Sent us a postcard from Big Ben just yesterday.”

We kept on reading. Despite the density of the material, it was really interesting to find out what my dad did for a living—other than fighting communists or bagging groceries.

“Woah, my dad found something that cured cancer!” I realized while reading one journal. Michael looked up.

“What? Really?”

“Oh, wait… it was found to have ten percent success rate in field mice, but ended up giving them two other types of cancer instead,” I sighed, “I guess we would have heard about it if he did.”

The pile of publications was running low, and I started to worry we won’t find anything at all. Each one I grab, hoping that at last this will be the one, but still nothing.

“The building is just leased by the university,” Michael read, “That’s disappointing. I was hoping for a malicious government agency. Or an organized crime syndicate?”

“You’re starting to sound like Kirk’s conspiracy theories,” I laughed, “Maybe it was a careless grad student?”

At last, I reached the last paper at the bottom of the bin. But I still didn’t feel like I knew anything new. Michael was still working on his end, so I head back to the computer to find some books on government projects during the war. There was no shortage of creative ideas to try and beat the Russians. Bats trained to carry bombs, sleeper spies, hypnotism embedded in propaganda to turn the enemies to the other side. There was no proof that my dad was a part of any of this. Besides, if it was published in a book at the library, it definitely wasn’t top secret. My dad would be able to talk about this.

Michael finally tossed his last paper to the side, rubbing his eyes. “There’s nothing here.”

Looking down at the papers, I realize that Michael might be right. After a whole afternoon of sifting through paper after paper, I didn’t learn anything that I didn’t already know.

Dusk is falling. My mother's probably overloaded, trying to do everything my father does around the house. She'll be expecting me home soon. But instead, I kept staring at the bin, as if it was going to reveal its secrets. Percy walks past me with a cart of encyclopedias and I stop him. "Dr. Warbler, are you sure this is all there is?" I asked, looking at the papers.

He slowed down the cart and nods. "That's all we have that is available to the public."

"The public? What does that mean?"

"The library will be closing in thirty minutes. I recommend you wrap your work up and check out any books you want to bring home." His friendly tone melted away in an instant. Before I could ask anything else, he resumed rolling his cart away.

I picked the bin up once again and start back down the rickety staircase to the cellar. I walk along the aisles of research, running my finger through the caked on dust on the edge of the shelf. It makes a brown line amongst a layer of grey. My father kept meticulous records of everything. But where else would they be?

When I returned back upstairs, my phone started ringing suddenly, and I pulled myself out of my thoughts. My mother was calling, and for some reason, even the ring tone seems more urgent than usual. “Hey, mom,” I whispered, careful not to disturb the people reading around me.

“Augustine! Why are you out so late? Where are you?” She sounded harried.

“I’m at the library,” I explained, taken aback, “Working on a project. What’s the matter?”

“I was just worried, that’s all. After everything that’s happened, with your father—if you’re going somewhere after school, just call me first, okay?”

I’ve been walking from school to home and around town alone for as long as I can remember. Her sudden need to know my whereabouts concerned me. After all, she didn’t think someone was going to kidnap me, does she? But there’s nothing stronger than the will of a mama bear, so I agree. “Okay, mom.”

“You need to come home right away.”

“What? Why?” Sure, the library was closing soon, but she had never been this strict when it came to curfew. In fact, I never had one.

“Elsie is almost finished with her famous macaroni and cheese, and she insists upon serving it steaming hot.”

“Elsie?” What was Aunt Elsie doing here? She visited once a year, where she went on a forty-eight hour insulting spree. Her last visit was only four months ago, and we still haven’t quite recovered. She insulted my father for not supporting his family, insulted me for not attending finishing school, and accidentally lit the tablecloth on fire all in one night.

My mother’s tone fell. “Since your father isn’t around, I needed someone to help with your brothers. Elsie and Frank seemed happy to accept the challenge.”

“Why did you pick Elsie? She hates us!” I whispered. I always had the fear that she might overhear me, and while I speak the truth, she doesn’t need another reason to hate me.

“I don’t know if that’s true. But I didn’t ask her. She volunteered,” my mother sighed, “It’s only temporary, darling. Until your father gets back.”

But my mom and I both know the prison sentence for arson can be five to ten years. Even though she hid it, I knew she was scared as I am.

"Okay mom, I'll be home soon. Can I bring a friend home with me?" I hardly realized I was saying the words until afterward. "It's important. We're in the middle of a homework project."

I picked the perfect lie. My mother couldn’t say no to me doing my homework. She loved homework. All professors do. At least, I think they do. She'll do anything to get me into the best college. And if Michael is with me, Elsie won’t be able to unleash her full fury on our family.

"Sure honey. I'll set an extra plate. Just get here before Trevor eats all of it," she said.

I shut my phone closed, hanging up, and turned to Michael. He listened to my half of the conversation intently. "So... Do you like macaroni and cheese?"

His mouth twitched into a smile. "It's my favorite."
