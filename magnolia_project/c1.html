Chapter 1

At Pepperbelly Creamery, there was a ice cream split you could order called the Pepperbelly Belly Breaker. The whole thing had about seven scoops of ice cream on it, along with two whole bananas and rivers of chocolate fudge. There was a list on the wall of those who have eaten the most, called the Belly Breaker’s Club. Basically, it was the world's longest, slowest eating contest. There was no definite period in which you win, or any sort of prize other than bragging rights, so the whole competition for a temporary title was pointless. It was the kind of think Kirk loved. He was number three on the list, having consumed 31 belly breakers over the course of his life. Number 32 was sitting in front of him, but that day, his phone was more interesting than his melting monstrosity of ice cream.

I stared at him, waiting to see how long it will be before he realized that I was there too. After all, I left the house and biked here in 90 degree weather to talk to him--not watch him read his phone.

"Kirk," I finally tried, and he picked up his spoon and started stirring it around in his bowl aimlessly, eyes still fixed on the phone. "Paging Captain Kirk. You're needed on the bridge."

"What?" he looked up at me for a moment, still hunched in his reading position. I looked to his ice cream mountain.

"It's melting," Pools of strawberry and vanilla ice cream started to mix together. Kirk stabbed his spoon into it and took a giant bite, but then resumed reading his phone. "Geez, what are you reading?"

"Just... a thing," Kirk lied. I grabbed his phone out of his hands, but he kept a tight grip on the sides. "Hey!"

"'BlackoutBunker.net. The news source the government doesn't want you to know about.'," I read off the top of his screen, "Seriously? This again?"

"Come on, I was just reading one article--"

"It's not news. I hope you don't take them seriously, the rantings of these crazed lunatics--"

"It was one article, seriously! I’m just trying to get some inspiration. If I could just have half the following this guy does, we’d be famous!"

Kirk started a website a few years ago called the Cedar Valley Underground. Back when we were freshmen with nothing better to do, we started investigating the small abandoned quarry on the edge of town. Over time, we found a connecting network of steam tunnels underneath the town. They were only supposed to be used for maintenance, and they were wicked hot and dangerous to wander around in. Kirk had the bright idea of cataloging our exploits online, and thus the Cedar Valley Underground was born. Kirk seemed to think that this online presence as a truth-seeker would earn him some street cred, but he was dead wrong. When he first advertised the website at our school, the backlash was bad enough to earn him the nickname "Sewer Boy", even though they were about steam tunnels and not sewers. In total, he only had about ten subscribers, and I’m not sure how many of them are actual people. Still, his flame couldn’t be extinguished, and he was always on the lookout for something that would really get him on the cover of Conspiracy Theories Weekly.

I just shrugged and scooped out a little more of my ice cream. "I don’t know why you idolize this crowd. At best, this guy is just coming up with lies for attention, and at worst, he honestly believes aliens live in his brain."

“BlackoutBunker isn’t about aliens. It’s hard-hitting, investigative journalism," when I don't hand back his phone, he tries out his big, baby-doll eyes. "Your dad is a scientist. I bet he saw all sorts of freaky stuff in secret government projects."

I reluctantly gave his phone back and scooped up the last dregs of my ice cream. My dad was a research scientist. But he didn’t work for the government; he’d been a research professor at the university for a while. But that was years ago, and he hasn’t done anything scientific since, except for random science projects in the shed. He worked at a grocery store, stacking shelves. My dad rarely talked about his old work. He loved his job, but one dispute with the university about an unpublished paper, and he threw in the towel. Turns out, even if you do something you love, there’s still miles of bureaucracy trying to separate you from it. Of course, I told Kirk this story, and he naturally jumped to conclusions—that he’d discovered something terrible and the university tried to get him to cover it up. A much better story, but ultimately, untrue.

"I’m going home," I tossed my cup into the trash can. Unlike Kirk, my family might actually pay attention to me.

No one locked their bikes in Cedar Valley, so I just wrenched my old silver bicycle out of the pile and took off. The city flew beneath my wheels. The faster I pedaled, the more the air around me moved, stirring up a breeze in the stagnant heat. I jumped my wheels over a curb and careened off to the right down the shortcut to my street. A dog walker leapt out of my way, tugging her corgi along with her. "Sorry!" I yelled, but by the time my voice reached her ears, I'm already around the next bend, headed towards the southern end of the university campus. As usual for the summer, the campus was bogged down with construction, and usually devoid of people. But something was different on the roads today. There was an unusual crowd standing in the street, milling about and talking amongst each other. At first, I just thought it was another campus fire drill, some freshman who forgot to add water to their mac n’ cheese. But this was way too many people for a fire drill, especially in the summer. I hopped off my bike to navigate the crowd on foot.

A fire? I quickened my pace, trying to push through the crowd to the street with my bike. Even though St. Clare Street is fairly wide, a mass of people are blocking my way. There’s a crowd of people gathered where a building stands—or at least, where it used to stand. All that remained was an charred brick shell. The glass in the windows shattered under the intense heat, leaving the holes in the walls staring like blinded eyes. The metal numbers on the door melted, and black scars cover the front facade. A firetruck stood at the scene, setting up barriers so the people watching can't get too close. Even in this ruined state, I recognized this building. It was a passing image of the past, a place I spent many afternoon in as a child, before my brothers were born and my dad quit work. It was the university’s neurology lab.

"What's going on? What happened?" I demanded to a passing fireman.

"Looks like an explosion," the fireman said, covered head to toe in fire retardant clothes.

"Was there anyone in there?"

The fireman shrugged. "Just let us do our job, kid."

A familiar voice distracted me, and I turned around to try and find the owner in the crowd. “Please, officers, you have to believe me—I had nothing to do with it! I was just passing by—visiting an old friend.”

“Terence Hall, you are under arrest for arson. Officer Stein will read you your rights.”

The crowd parted almost miraculously, and I could see my father clearly across the street, still wearing his green Shop n’ Save apron, trying to bargain furiously with the officers as they cuffed his hands together.

“Dad!” I ran to him, leaving my bicycle behind in the dust, “What’s going on?”

“Nothing to worry about, Grace,” he assured me, “It’s all one big misunderstanding. Just run home and tell your mother she’ll be expecting a phone call from me soon,” he looked to the officers booking him, “we still get phone calls, right?”

“Dad, this isn’t some kind of joke!” I yelled at him, “Why are you arresting him? What did my dad do?”

Miss, your father set that building on fire,” one of the police officers told me. The acrid smell of burning bricks saturated the air. My dad didn’t seem bothered by the news. I knew he was being brave because of me. But I wish, just for once, he would be human. Get angry or get sad or do something other than just accept what the world threw at him.

"It's going to be okay," he promised me, staring at the building. Still smoking, flames still danced in the empty windows, “I promise, I'll be right back."

I reluctantly let go and wiped the snot trailing from my nostril. My eyes shook with teardrops. The police officer ducked my father’s head as he took a seat in the back of the police cruiser.

I biked home as fast as I could, swerving slightly on the roads. Despite my detour, I managed to make it home about the same time I always did. My mother was in the living room, grading papers. She smiled when she saw me.

“Grace, good to see you back,” she said. Even in the summer semester, she was knee-deep in papers to grade, “You haven’t seen your father, have you? He should be home by now.”

After I tell her the news, my mother hovered by the phone. She carried it around with her throughout the house. She was the queen of self-containment, the maestro of the household, the one who keeps all the stitches of our family from pulling apart. Not anymore; this was the first time I have ever seen her so undone. Her hair fell out of her bun in small strands, and she wore her slippers around the house. I didn’t know who she’s waiting for a phone call from, but when she did call people, her conversations were frustrated.

“What do you mean, no bail until Monday?” she demanded. I decided to make dinner that night: pasta, since it was one of the few things I could cook without help and she seemed otherwise occupied. “Terry would never hurt a fly. You know him. No, I don’t care about the circumstances.”

My brothers and I sat down to eat in silence. Max picked at his food with his fork, watching the pasta bits slide off. They were a little mushier than al dente. I kept looking over my shoulder to see if my mother would join us, but she locked herself in the study to talk away from listening ears. I ended up boxing her meal up and putting it in the fridge. Maybe she would eat later.

“I absolutely refuse to believe it. Sure, it used to be his old lab, but he hasn’t set foot in there in years. Why would he want to blow it up, of all things?”

“I cleaned up the kitchen and the boys are in bed,” I told her. They seemed disturbed by the lack of Dad’s presence, or the solid sense of order and family our house always seems to have. Used to have.

“Not now, love,” she held up one of her fingers. “I want to have a word with the police chief, but they keep rerouting me through endless phone trees.”

I backed out of the room, slowly towards the stairs. The house felt empty without him.
