Chapter 2

News traveled fast. Faster than I thought it would. But of course it did; it was the second week of high school, the second week of my senior year. Everyone had already finished catching up on summer plans and discussing who lost weight and who got tan, and there weren’t yet any tests to stress about. Nothing else to talk about except for that fearsome arsonist who’d decided to wreck havoc in our pleasant Virginia town. And of course, his daughter, who attended their school. Me.

The hallway's normal flow of traffic bent and warped around me as I walked. Kids once oblivious to my existence turned and stared as I passed. Even in the most crowded hallway, I could walk in the very center without rubbing elbows with a single human being. The problem was, I could hear the whispers. My fellow students didn’t know how easy it is for me to hear their conversations. Every single one was about me.

“Yeah, I heard her dad burned down the building.”

“My dad’s a police officer, and he was at the station that day. He was totally unhinged.”

“How does someone snap like that?”

“There were fifteen people inside. Thank God the semester hadn’t started.”

“Oh great, arson and attempted murder.”

Heat rose in my cheeks and I kept my head down, marching as quickly as I could forwards. I didn’t look where I was going, all I knew was I wanted to escape the crowds, the watching eyes, the whispers--

Kirk joined up with me as he came from the English hall. Unlike the stolen glances of my peers, he looked directly at me and smiled. And while everyone else swerved to avoid me, he entered the bubble, turning to match my pace along the hallway.

“Hey there, friend,” he said, his tone cordial. I turned my face away so he can’t see how red my eyes were. “What’s the matter? Are you alright?”

“Haven’t you heard?” My voice shook with every syllable, “Apparently, my dad’s a pyromaniac psychopath. And since I’m his daughter, I’m a bomb just waiting to explode,” I tightened my grip on my backpack, and my knuckles turned white with the strain.

Kirk waved a hand, as if it was an issue easily dismissed. “They’re idiots. Don’t listen to them.”

“How can I not, when it’s all I hear?” I couldn’t keep my tears in my eyes. They kept sneaking forwards, into my eyelids, right on the precipice.

We arrived at the door to chemistry class and took our usual seats, but I kept my head bowed so no one could look at me directly. Chemistry was usually the class I looked forward to. Our teacher, Ms. Blackburn, rarely lectured; she’d said the first day of class that it was better to set things on fire than learn about them through books. Normally, that’s the kind of energy I’d look forward in a class. But the mere mention of fire today my stomach turn. I quietly said a prayer to myself in my head: please let this be a boring lecture day. Please let us learn about oxidation or surface tension so I don’t have to talk to another human being.

My prayer must not have gotten the reception it needed in our science classroom, however, because Ms. Blackburn marched to the front of the classroom immediately after the bell to get us back on our feet. “Everyone: lab partners, goggles, head to the back lab stations. If your hair is any longer than Aiden’s, I need it tied up and out of your face! We are working with the Bunsen burners today!” Immediately, everyone started partnering up, rushing around the room to sit next to their best friend. Kirk and I walked to the back together, selecting a station at random. Laminated instructions were sitting next to the crucible and the bunsen burner, and I pretended to be fascinated by them. Beside me, Kirk pulled his goggles on his face and snapped them, recoiling when they hit him in the face.

“Excuse me?” I felt someone tap on my shoulder. Her big brown eyes took up half her face and her bushy black hair took up half of the room. When she spoke, her voice was small, either because she was shy or she thought it was more appealing to the ear. “Do you mind if… I work with him?”

“Who, Kirk?” I spat out his name like a dead fish. He was rubbing his eye where the goggles had slammed into his face.

“Kirk… yeah,” she said his name like she was savoring it. I looked over to Kirk to exchange a disbelieving smirk, but he was staring at her, his mouth slightly agape. The girl was dressed completely in yellow, complete with yellow raincoat and yellow flip-flops.

“It’s cool. We can… we can work together,” he said to her, and leaned an arm on the table. His eye was still red from the goggles. I stared at him, trying to brand the idea of betrayal on his forehead. So much for having my best friend by my side.

“No more than two to a lab station, please!” Blackburn insisted, and I know she’s talking about us.

“We can work over here. Come with me,” the girl led Kirk away to a station down the row, and he followed after her, trying to keep the drool from coming out of his mouth. Great. Now where was I supposed to find a lab partner who wouldn’t act like I was going to burn the whole room down?

There was only one person left who didn’t have a partner. He was a boy only a little taller than me, with brown hair in an unremarkable bowl cut. I remember Ms. Blackburn reading his name on the role call on the first day and noticing that he was new to our school, but I’d already forgotten his name. He’d been bouncing between lab partners left and right for the last week, but I didn’t have the chance to make his acquaintance yet. He’d made a reputation for himself as a bit of a klutz, which is why no one wanted to work with him. He spilled hydrochloric acid on Kimberly’s volleyball uniform before her big game. He almost got expelled on the first day for bring matches to school. Even now, he was examining the striker at his lab station with a curious intensity, holding it a little too close to his hair. If he set his head on fire while I was his lab partner, well… the optics would not be good. Still, it could be worse. If he was new to our school, he probably didn’t know about my dad, or anything about me at all.

“Hi. I’m Grace Hall. I guess we’re lab partners,” I said, trying to sound approachable—or at the very least, neutral. “I’m sorry, I don’t remember your name.”

He put down the striker and held out a hand for me to shake. “Michael McKenna. Hi,” he flashed a bright, wide smile at me, “So did your dad really burn down a building?”


It was a bit of a tight fit to make it to visiting hours after school ended, so my mom picked me up from school early on Thursday. I was surprised she wanted to bring me at all; she refused point-blank to allow my brothers to come with. Partly because prison is a scary place, partly because she’s afraid they’ll like it too much and become career criminals. I think since I was the one to see him arrested, she thinks I’ll get closure this way. The little girl in me wanted to curl up next to him on the old leather armchair.

As we drove through town, I looked out the window and saw all of the children playing in the street. They acted as if every day was as beautiful as this.

"All right, Grace?" She asked me as I leaned my head against the window, trying to soak up the sunshine through the glass.

"Yeah, I'm alright," I said, and squirmed in my seat. My sweatshirt is a little tight around my neck for some reason, and I tugged at the neckline so I could breathe. Not everything was alright. I shut my eyes tight against the world and swallowed my frustrations away. It usually went away after a few minutes, and I had to keep my head screwed on tight if I wanted to get through today. Wouldn't want to fly off the handle in public.

My mother sat at the steering wheel with her shoulders hunched up kind of high. She didn't look very comfortable, either. She kept her eyes on the road, but seemed to be struggling with something. I could only see the side of her head, where her blonde hair was starting to turn ashy along the sides. "Are you all right, mom?" I asked, watching her.

"Me? Yeah, yeah, I'm fine," she shifted side to side in her seat. She didn't look any better than I am. My mother was never uncomfortable. She never faltered when she had to talk to me about other, serious things, like about drugs and college and boyfriends. When my pet hermit crab died when I was seven, she explained what happened with a cool, emotional detachment. "Listen, Grace... A few people in the community are mounting a legal defense fund for your father. There are people that support him. We need to be there for him, too."

"I know," she was clearly struggling with this conversation, so I offer her a way out. Besides, she forgets that I'm seventeen now, a month away from being an adult. I don't need my mom to tell me about the ups and downs life brings. I think I’m more emotionally prepared for this than she.

"Please let me finish," she warned me, and turned left down the road the prison is on, “No matter what happens—innocent or guilty—we will be there for him.”

“Of course. But he’s not guilty.”

My mother pursed her lips together, and I leaned my head back against the window. I alternated between wanting to be here and regretting even coming. If I was the one driving the car, I'm not entirely sure that I would have ever left the driveway. She probably wanted to warn me about believing in my father’s innocence—but I knew for a fact, he was. He told me so. It’s all just a misunderstanding, Grace. That’s what he said.

Suddenly, the prison appeared before us, a square, foreboding place. The cinder block walls were grey and white, and it sat like a pockmark of ugly against the empty field around it. They checked our licenses when we get to the gate, confirmed our reason for visiting. I think it’s ironic that the two places most commonly seen with gate patrols are prisons and fancy housing developments. The world is surprisingly circular sometimes.

After the gate, I sat around for almost twenty minutes—it turns out, the preliminary paperwork wasn’t even close to enough. We walked through metal detectors and got patted down. I had to leave my school backpack in the car; I didn’t have anything in my pockets except for a pack of gum, but even that stirred intense debate. The guards finally decided that I could bring it in.

Even after all the waiting, I was still on pins and needles to see my dad. At last, after they couldn’t find any other reason to not let us in, the doors open.

I stood in the doorway, scanning the visiting room for my father’s face. It wasn’t like how they show it in the movies, with phones along the wall. There are small tables set up around the room, loved ones speaking quietly to each other. Guards stood in every corner, motionless but watching. The other door opened and a man steps out, followed by a guard. Orange was not my father’s color. It made him look reddish and flushed. His hair was still dark and full for his age, but the grey streaks in the side stand out more than ever. But still, I’ve never been so happy to see him in my life.

“Dad!” I immediately ran towards him, dodging tables and chairs. I wanted to give him a hug, but the guards cleared their throats and I stopped myself just in time. Spray-painted on the wall next to us, it said NO TOUCHING. Just because there wasn’t a plastic window between us doesn’t mean he’s any closer.

“Let’s have a seat, shall we love?” he offered, his voice the same as it ever was. If anything, he seemed a little tired. My mother waited beside an open table, and I completely forgot that she was standing there. I returned to the table and sat in a calm and orderly fashion, focused on my feet. Dad sat next to me. My mom volunteered to grab some snacks out of the vending machine, a suggestion that my father practically salivates over. The food here probably isn’t palatable. The few moments she leaves gave me the chance to have some privacy with my father.

“I miss you so much,” I said, my words tumbling out over each other. I didn’t know how much time we have, and I had so much to say. “Home isn’t the same without you.”

“I miss you too,” he said. The orange reflects in his glasses, giving his eyes a strange, carrot-like glow.

“What were you doing at the old lab, dad?” I asked him, “Why did they arrest you?”

“Oh, who knows how the police make the decisions that they make. Maybe it was the two eyewitnesses who said they saw me leaving the building before the explosion. I was in the wrong place at the wrong time.”

"This isn’t how the justice system is supposed to work! They can’t just pick someone at random to blame and throw in jail.”

“Unfortunately, it’s not always as black and white as who set fire to a building,” he explained.

“But there’s no evidence! They can’t hold you here—”

“I’m sure the police will find whatever evidence they want. I’m sure they’ll see that shed full of chemicals we have from that old science fair project and decide I was building a bomb. Or they’ll find the building’s swipe-access log and assume that because I did not have swipe access to the building, I must have stolen an ID to cover my tracks,” he said, his tone patient, “That’s enough to hold me as long as they want.”

“Are you saying someone is framing you?”

“Please, love, don’t jump to conclusions,” he shook his head. “All I’m saying is… not everyone is my best friend, despite what you believe. I am not a perfect man.”

“There were fifteen other people in that building.”

“Grace, I might not be able to give you all the details you want, but I can say with certainty that what happened here was not an attempted murder. The other people being in the building—that was a mistake. I want you to know that you’re safe. No one is going to hurt you. As long as you continue to trust in the right people.”

“And what about you? Are you going to be okay?” I asked. He puffs out his chest.

“Don’t worry about me. Worry about climate change.”

“Dad, stop joking—”

“Stay strong for me. I love you,” he said, and nodded his head to my mother, who had returned. “Give me a moment to speak alone with your mother?”

“Yeah. Love you too, dad,” and with that, I turned away just in case tears start coming out of my eyes. My mother returned, rested a hand on my shoulder. How could my dad fight in a war, but not fight for himself? The way he sat there now. With a smile on his face. Was he being brave, by not acting out against those who bully him? Or was he too cowardly to speak for himself?

I walked out of the room to the waiting room, where a few decrepit vending machines decorated the barren hallway. At the grocery, he served hundreds of people a day, always with a smile on his face. As if he was put on this Earth for no other purpose than to make their day a little brighter. My father had more friends than stars in the sky. He treated everyone with the same warmth and compassion. Unless there’s someone we didn’t know. My dad could make friends with anyone. But the Gregory County Municipal Prison? I could only hope so.
