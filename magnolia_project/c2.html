Chapter 2

School was as foreign to me as another country the next day. News traveled fast. Faster than I thought it would. The hallway's normal flow of traffic bent and warped around me. Kids once oblivious to my existence turned and stared as I passed. Even in the most crowded hallway, I could walk in the very center without rubbing elbows with a single human being. The problem was, I could hear the whispers. My fellow students didn’t know how easy it is for me to hear their conversations. Every single one was about me.

“Yeah, I heard her dad burned down the building.”

“My dad’s on the force. They brought him in Friday. He was totally unhinged.”

“How does someone snap like that?”

“Do you think she could do the same?”

Heat rose in my cheeks and I kept my head down, marching as quickly as I could forwards. I didn’t look where I was going, all I knew was I wanted to escape the crowds, the watching eyes, the whispers--

Kirk walked towards me. Unlike the stolen glances of my peers, he looked directly at me and smiles. And while everyone else swerved to avoid me, he entered the bubble, turning to match my pace along the hallway.

“Hey there, friend,” he said, his tone cordial. I turned my face away so he can’t see how red my eyes were. “What’s the matter? Are you alright?”

“Haven’t you heard?” My voice shook with every syllable, “Apparently, my dad’s a pyromaniac psychopath. And since I’m his daughter, I’m a bomb just waiting to explode,” I tightened my grip on my backpack, and my knuckles turned white with the strain.

Kirk waved a hand, as if it was an issue easily dismissed. “They’re idiots. Don’t listen to them.”

“How can I not, when it’s all I hear?” I couldn’t keep my tears in my eyes. They kept sneaking forwards, into my eyelids, right on the precipice. We arrived at the door to chemistry class and took our usual seats, but I kept my head bowed so no one could look at me directly.

“Everyone: lab partners, goggles, head to the back lab stations. If your hair is any longer than Aiden’s, I need it tied up and out of your face! We are working with the Bunsen burners today!” Our teacher, Ms. Blackburn, marched across the front of the classroom. Immediately, everyone started partnering up, rushing around the room to sit next to their best friend. Kirk and I walked to the back together, selecting a station at random. I picked up the instructions and began to scan them. Finally, something to distract me. Today was going to be fine, I kept reminding myself. After all, I had my friend by my side. Nothing was going to go wrong.

“Excuse me?” I felt someone tap on my shoulder. Her big brown eyes took up half her face and her bushy black hair took up half of the room. When she spoke, her voice is small, either because she was shy or she thought it was more appealing to the ear. “Do you mind if… I work with him?”

“Who, Kirk?” I spat out his name like a dead fish. No one ever wants to work with Kirk. That’s what makes him such a reliable lab partner.

“Kirk… yeah,” she said his name like she’s savoring it. I looked over to Kirk to exchange a disbelieving smirk, but he was staring at her, his mouth slightly agape. The girl was dressed completely in yellow, complete with yellow raincoat and yellow flip-flops.

“It’s cool. We can… we can work together,” he said to her. I stared at him, trying to brand the idea of betrayal on his forehead. So much for having my best friend by my side.

“No more than two to a lab station, please!” Blackburn insisted, and I know she’s talking about us.

“We can work over here. Come with me,” the girl led Kirk away to a station down the row, and he followed after her, trying to keep the drool from coming out of his mouth. Great. Now where was I supposed to find a lab partner who won’t burn the whole room down?

I didn’t even finish rolling my eyes before I saw someone standing next to me. He held the striker in his hand, investigating it closely.

“Who are you?” I asked. I didn’t know his name, only that he was the new kid, and he’s been bouncing between lab partners left and right since school started. He was a bit of a klutz, and no one wanted to work with him. He almost set his own hair on fire with a bunsen burner. He spilled hydrochloric acid on Kimberly’s volleyball uniform before a big game. He almost got expelled on the first day for bringing matches to school.

“Michael McKenna. Hi,” he flashed a bright, wide smile at me, “So did your dad really burn down a building?”


It was a bit of a tight fit to make it to visiting hours after school ended, so my mom picked me up from school early on Thursday. I was surprised she wanted to bring me at all; she refused point-blank to allow my brothers to come with. Partly because prison is a scary place, partly because she’s afraid they’ll like it too much and become career criminals. I think since I was the one to see him arrested, she thinks I’ll get closure this way. The little girl in me wanted to curl up next to him on the old leather armchair.

As we drove through town, I looked out the window and saw all of the children playing in the street. They acted as if every day was as beautiful as this.

"All right, Augustine?" She asked me as I leaned my head against the window, trying to soak up the sunshine through the glass.

"Yeah, I'm alright," I said, and squirmed in my seat. My sweatshirt is a little tight around my neck for some reason, and I tugged at the neckline so I could breathe. Not everything was alright. I shut my eyes tight against the world and swallowed my frustrations away. It usually went away after a few minutes, and I had to keep my head screwed on tight if I wanted to get through today. Wouldn't want to fly off the handle in public.

My mother sat at the steering wheel with her shoulders hunched up kind of high. She didn't look very comfortable, either. She kept her eyes on the road, but seemed to be struggling with something. I could only see the side of her head, where her blonde hair was starting to turn ashy along the sides. "Are you all right, mom?" I asked, watching her.

"Me? Yeah, yeah, I'm fine," she shifted side to side in her seat. She didn't look any better than I am. My mother was never uncomfortable. She never faltered when she had to talk to me about other, serious things, like about drugs and college and boyfriends. When my pet hermit crab died when I was seven, she explained what happened with a cool, emotional detachment. "Listen, Augustine... A few people in the community are mounting a legal defense fund for your father. There are people that support him. We need to be there for him, too."

"I know," she was clearly struggling with this conversation, so I offer her a way out. Besides, she forgets that I'm seventeen now, a month away from being an adult. I don't need my mom to tell me about the ups and downs life brings. I think I’m more emotionally prepared for this than she.

"Please let me finish," she warned me, and turned left down the road the prison is on, “No matter what happens—innocent or guilty—we will be there for him.”

“Of course. But he’s not guilty.”

My mother pursed her lips together, and I leaned my head back against the window. I alternated between wanting to be here and regretting even coming. If I was the one driving the car, I'm not entirely sure that I would have ever left the driveway. She probably wanted to warn me about believing in my father’s innocence—but I knew for a fact, he was. He told me so. It’s all just a misunderstanding, Augustine. That’s what he said.

Suddenly, the prison appeared before us, a square, foreboding place. The cinder block walls were grey and white, and it sat like a pockmark of ugly against the empty field around it. They checked our licenses when we get to the gate, confirmed our reason for visiting. I think it’s ironic that the two places most commonly seen with gate patrols are prisons and fancy housing developments. The world is surprisingly circular sometimes.

After the gate, I sat around for almost twenty minutes—it turns out, the preliminary paperwork wasn’t even close to enough. We walked through metal detectors and got patted down. I had to leave my school backpack in the car; I didn’t have anything in my pockets except for a pack of gum, but even that stirred intense debate. The guards finally decided that I could bring it in.

Even after all the waiting, I was still on pins and needles to see my dad. At last, after they couldn’t find any other reason to not let us in, the doors open.

I stood in the doorway, scanning the visiting room for my father’s face. It wasn’t like how they show it in the movies, with phones along the wall. There are small tables set up around the room, loved ones speaking quietly to each other. Guards stood in every corner, motionless but watching. The other door opened and a man steps out, followed by a guard. Orange was not my father’s color. It made him look reddish and flushed. His hair was still dark and full for his age, but the grey streaks in the side stand out more than ever. But still, I’ve never been so happy to see him in my life.

“Dad!” I immediately ran towards him, dodging tables and chairs. I wanted to give him a hug, but the guards cleared their throats and I stopped myself just in time. Spray-painted on the wall next to us, it said NO TOUCHING. Just because there wasn’t a plastic window between us doesn’t mean he’s any closer.

“Let’s have a seat, shall we love?” he offered, his voice the same as it ever was. If anything, he seemed a little tired. My mother waited beside an open table, and I completely forgot that she was standing there. I returned to the table and sat in a calm and orderly fashion, focused on my feet. Dad sat next to me. My mom volunteered to grab some snacks out of the vending machine, a suggestion that my father practically salivates over. The food here probably isn’t palatable. The few moments she leaves gave me the chance to have some privacy with my father.

“I miss you so much,” I said, my words tumbling out over each other. I didn’t know how much time we have, and I had so much to say. “Home isn’t the same without you.”

“I miss you too,” he said. The orange reflects in his glasses, giving his eyes a strange, carrot-like glow.

“What were you doing at the old lab, dad?” I asked him, “What were you thinking?”

“I guess I wasn’t,” he sighed, “But some of these things don’t have to do with thinking. Sometimes you’re just in the wrong place at the wrong time.”

“This isn’t how the justice system is supposed to work! They don’t just throw an innocent man in jail—we have to fight it.”

“Unfortunately, it’s not always as black and white as who set fire to a building,” he explained.

“But there’s no evidence! They can’t hold you here—”

“The police are going to go to our house and they are going to find a shed full of very flammable chemicals,” he said, his tone patient, “That’s enough to hold me as long as they want.”

“Are you saying someone framed you?”

“Please, love, don’t jump to conclusions,” he shook his head. “All I’m saying is… not everyone is my best friend, despite what you believe. I am not a perfect man.”

“Does this have to do with your work with the government? What did you do, to make people hate you so much?”

“You know I can’t tell you what I did. Treason has a much longer sentence than arson,” he said, a sad smile on his face.

“Are you sure you’re okay?” I ask. He puffs out his chest.

“Don’t worry about me. Worry about climate change.”

“Dad, stop joking—”

“Take care of your mother for me. I love you,” My dad could make friends with anyone. But the Gregory County Municipal Prison? I could only hope so.

“Love you too, dad,” and with that, I turned away just in case tears start coming out of my eyes. My mother returned, rested a hand on my shoulder. How could my dad fight in a war, but not fight for himself? The way he sat there now. With a smile on his face. Was he being brave, by not acting out against those who bully him? Or was he too cowardly to speak for himself?

I walked out of the room to the waiting room, where a few decrepit vending machines decorated the barren hallway. At the grocery, he served hundreds of people a day, always with a smile on his face. As if he was put on this Earth for no other purpose than to make their day a little brighter. My father had more friends than stars in the sky. He treated everyone with the same warmth and compassion. Unless there’s someone we didn’t know. But except for his work, my father didn’t keep secrets from us.
