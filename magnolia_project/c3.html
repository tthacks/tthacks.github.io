Chapter 3

Who would have thought that Kirk would be the first of us to have a date? In fact, since that first Chemistry class, I haven’t seen him apart from that Christie girl. They’ve walked to classes together, eaten lunch together… sitting at Pepperbelly Creamery, this is the first time I’ve actually seen him alone. Instead of staring at his phone today, he’s staring dreamily at the ceiling like they’ve posted a picture of her up there. “Man, Christie is just amazing. She’s so funny, and smart, and her hair smells like mandarin oranges. Why don’t they make men’s shampoo smell so good?”

I considered making a snappy remark about how women’s and men’s shampoo work the same and it’s all about preference and branding, but I don’t think he would get it. “I don’t know, Kirk. I guess it’s just one of life’s little mysteries.”

“Of course, it’s not all about me. How’s your new lab partner? That Mitchell guy?”

“Michael,” I corrected him, and then immediately dismissed the subject. “He’s fine.”

Actually, I didn’t want to get into the subject of my new lab partner. I was wary of him at first, since he seemed to give off intense homeschool vibes, like he’d never talked to another human his age in his life. After asking me point-blank if my father burned down a building, he only got stranger. He didn’t have any friends that I knew of. We had a fire drill two days ago, and he spent the whole time lying in the grass, laughing. He refused to go back inside. He looks at the world with wide eyes, as if he’s seeing everything for the very first time. Like he just stepped off a spaceship. Or maybe he’s stoned?

“That’s good. I feel totally bad for abandoning you, but I’m glad it’s all working out,” immediately, his phone lit up—a text message. He dropped everything to pick it up and type a response. I sighed and slumped in my seat to look out the window, “By the way, Christie’s coming by in like, thirty seconds,” Kirk threw his phone to the side. I immediately straightened up.

“What?” The last thing I wanted to see was Lovey-Dovey Kirk—somehow, it was more infuriating than Conspiracy Kirk. Besides, the point of getting ice cream every week was to unwind, and I knew this was just going to set me on edge.

“Yeah, I invited her. It’s cool, right?” he asked, and slid to the end of the booth. No, it’s not cool at all, I thought, but the front bell was already jingling behind me. Without even turning around, I knew it was her by the way Kirk’s face changed. He immediately stood up and ran his fingers through his orange hair to ensure the most shocking appearance possible. Christie was dressed in Pepto-Bismol pink with a matching beret planted on her bushy hair. She spotted him and scuttled over to our booth, planting an enthusiastic kiss on his cheek.

“Christie, you know Grace, of course,” he said, gesturing to me. I still sat in my booth, trying not to scowl. The wave of mandarin-orange scent hit my nose like a wall. She didn’t just use orange shampoo, she used orange conditioner and orange perfume and possibly even orange hair-spray. I made a mental note to check my shampoo later to make sure I never smell as revoltingly sweet as she did. I nodded hello and she took a seat beside Kirk. They squeezed in so close to each other, you would think the edges of the booth were lava. Kirk was the first to talk. “I was hoping you two could get to know each other.”

"What’s to know?" I said curtly, not even looking at Christie. I was mad at Kirk for inviting her without asking me, but what’s new? Christie’s big eyes pop at the sight of Kirk’s Belly Breaker split.

“That’s the most ice cream I’ve ever seen in my life!” she gasped, “Are you really going to eat all of it?”

“I might need your help,” Kirk said, taking a spoonful and feeding it to Christie. I shuddered and rolled my eyes.

“You seemed to do just fine on the first thirty-two,” I said, referring to his record, “And you’ll be disqualified if you share.”

“It’s fine. I have a big enough lead for now. No one will catch up to me,” he said. This is the same kid who forced me to come with him every week in the middle of the winter just to watch him eat one of these dumb things. It was the coldest winter on record, but he was afraid his competition would try and get an edge on him.

Christie batted her eyes and swallowed the ice cream. “I’m so sorry to hear about all that stuff with your dad, Grace. But I bet you’ll get an amazing college entrance essay out of it. Colleges love students they can take pity on.”

I felt my skin turn red hot, despite the chilly ice cream. This was clearly supposed to be some kind of olive branch, but Christie was too much of an air-headed idiot to realize she was slapping me in the face with it. I slammed my spoon into my bowl with a little too much force and it bent on the bottom.

“Well, Christie, I don’t really think my father’s wrongful incarceration is really any of their business,” I said.

She smiled brightly, continuing to miss the hint. “Oh, well I’m on the journalism team at school, and they always teach us that you can spin any story. I’m sure you can find some way to sell it as a challenge you overcame!”

Kirk straightened up in his seat at the mention of journalism. “Oh, that reminds me, Grace. Christie started writing some articles for the Cedar Valley Underground,” he said, “And she’s got a real knack for journalism, because the head writer for BlackoutBunker.net contacted me last week and said he’s really interested in some of the stuff we’ve been putting up. Especially the article I wrote on the fire, and all the arson allegations—a wronged scientist burning down a building? How cool is that?”

Kirk queued up another spoonful for his girlfriend. I couldn’t stop myself; I grabbed the spoon out of his hand and threw it on the ground.

“What the hell?” he asked, arm still raised. I slammed my hands on the table and got in his face.

“You can write all the bullshit you want on your stupid website. But if you spread one false rumor about my father—”

“I haven’t made up anything. I’m just stating the facts as they were. Come on, Grace. You’re being insufferable.”

Christie cowered a little bit, but I didn’t relent. “Then say the truth: my father was framed. And to make it even better, I’m going to prove my father’s innocence. And write about it. Then they’ll publish my work in an newspaper, and not a God-damn Wordpress blog with the default template!”

With that, I stood up from my chair and toss my ice cream cup into the trash. Before he could argue, I made a beeline for the door, not waiting to see if my low grade insults made a dent on his massive ego.
