Chapter 8

A crowd of police cars blocked my driveway when I got home. They cluttered almost the entire street, and I had to hop off my bike just to weave through them. People dressed in navy police windbreakers buzzed around in the kitchen.

“That’s not evidence,” my mother was in the front yard, arguing with one of them, “I agreed to a search, but seriously?”

In the man’s hand was a family photo from our foyer, wrapped up in a sterile plastic bag.

“Sorry, ma’am. We’re going to need to take this,” her fingers slipped over the plastic bag and the man pulled it away from her. My mother looks more lost than I have ever seen her. For one thing, she’s wearing her slippers in the grass.

“What’s going on, mom?” I looked around at the forensics workers bustling around us, ignoring us as if we were lawn statues.

“They needed to collect evidence. I thought being cooperative would help his case, but I’m really losing my patience with these gremlins,” she ran her fingers through her knotty blonde hair, “They’re robbing our house of everything, claiming it’s relevant to the case.”

“We’ll get it all back when Dad’s released,” I assured her. I knew it wasn’t the things she cares about; it was just the idea of losing him. Of losing what sanity she and our family has left. Maybe they would haul Aunt Elsie away too. Unfortunately for me, it was unlikely they would find her useful for their case, except for maybe a character witness for the prosecution. I see two men carrying away boxes from the second floor; Dad’s files from his study.

“Hey!” I ran up to the box, which takes two men to carry. They strain slightly under the weight of it, “Why are you taking that?”

“It’s evidence,” he said, “Please, get out of our way.”

“But—” I quickly shut my mouth, realizing I didn’t have much of an argument. I wasn’t finished going through those. What if there was something important about the Magnolia Project that I hadn’t found yet? They wouldn’t be any help to anyone, sitting in an evidence locker in the precinct. The men didn’t wait for me to finish my sentence, though. They just plowed on past me, throwing my father’s work into the back of the truck with the rest of the stuff.

“Hey, Rodriguez!” One of the men near the shed beckoned to his friend. “Come check this out.” He opened up the door to the shed, where my Dad used to keep some chemicals—mostly for cleaning, of course, but he was a scientist, and sometimes he got a little creative with his chemistry. I know he’s never done anything malicious with them, but to their eyes, my father’s collection of Bunsen burners and beakers looks very much like the factory for a bomb. Now I know what he meant by looking guilty. They’re going to find some combination of those chemicals that will make a bomb—and that’s how they’ll frame him.

“He’s innocent!” I yelled at the men, as they loaded up the truck, “This isn’t justice!”

My mother rested her hands on my shoulders, as if to keep me from chasing after them. She smoothed my hair around my neck. “Head up to your room, Grace. You’re better off staying out of their way.” She never sounded more defeated in her life.

I stormed upstairs and locked myself in my room, but I was determined not to rest. I opened up my laptop and start searching for the Magnolia Project. A website for a women’s health group in Atlanta came up, but nothing else particularly interesting. I get more search results looking photos for meerkats in space.

Disappointed, I thought for a moment of closing my computer, but then another thought occurred to me. Sure, I still had a lot of resentment about Kirk and the way he treated me. And I definitely didn’t want to ask him for help. But he had a skill, a skill for not giving up even when all evidence told him he was wrong. I was only looking at one side of the Internet. If Kirk wanted to know about something blasphemous, where would he look?

I opened up an untracked browser (I seriously did NOT need this on my search history) and searched for the news source the government… oh gosh I couldn’t even think it… the news source the government didn’t want me to know about. BlackoutBunker.net.

The website was… well, to put it nicely, it looked like a labor of love by someone who probably had never seen another website in their life. The look only varied slightly from the default HTML style by putting white text on a black background, and the occasional, very-pixelated header. And when it came to trying to actually find specific content on the site, I doubted the website owner considered the layout of content when he was stringing this series of webpages together. The only way to find things was to click through link after link after link, sometimes hidden in the middle of stories about government projects during World War II where bats were trained to carry bombs, missing persons cases from the early 2000s, and how computers were going to ruin bird migration patterns. And yet, through all of this, I nearly jumped out of my chair when I found the headline.

EVERYTHING WE KNOW ABOUT THE MAGNOLIA PROJECT

A BlackoutBunker exclusive - by into_thin_air

Over the years, truth-seekers and believers have come to this website to learn of the strange and macabre, to listen to and engage my take on supernatural phenomena that often goes overlooked. And while it has been my honor as editor-in-chief to bring this information to you, today I write not about speculation or rumor but a cause near and dear to my heart.

For as long as humanity has possessed the power of thought, we have kept secrets. In business, in relationships, in all matters public and private, we hide behind personas and lie for a thousand different reasons. Inside the mind is the only place where one can truly feel at home, and yet there exist talented humans who can breach even that barrier. Generations have called them psychics, empaths, mind-readers and mediums. And while their work has often been decried as a scam by many, the United States government would be damned if they were to let even the possibility of such a powerful weapon go untested. After all, in the words of Sun Tzu, to know one’s enemy is to know oneself, and how better could one know one’s enemy than to know their deepest intentions?

Shortly after the end of the Cold War, in the height of the McCarthy era, there was a new witch hunt afoot in the United States. Psychics were being rounded up around the country to study their abilities. And while the excitement and funding for this project began to fizzle out around the time of the fall of the Iron Curtain, there remains a dedicated group of scientists who believe that these individuals will be useful in service to their country. In fact, it continued to run steadily through the early 2000s, until a tragic accident at a research facility buried the project in so much ethical red tape that the researchers feared the possible scandal would never outweigh the benefits of this middling project, and quickly disbanded it.

Impassioned reader, if you believe nothing else on this website, believe this. Believe the hundreds that participated, who know that given any opportunity or inspiration, the government will find them again. Because what if I told you that it didn’t stop there? What if I told you that it was more than just mind tricks, that there was something else out there, some greater power that the human mind cannot imagine? The government only gave up on this project because they lost their creativity—what if they discovered something far more dangerous in the process?

If you or someone you know was involved in these experiments, please contact me immediately and share your story. It is more important than you know.

When I finished reading the article, I leaned back in my desk chair, ruminating in the quiet, desperate passion of the author. It was ridiculous, of course. Scientists didn’t waste their time with psychics. And this person clearly didn’t understand what it meant to be a scientist, because they never cited a single source or explained their ominous conclusion. And as for the call to action… into_thin_air (Who must be the world’s biggest Jon Krakauer fan) might as well asked for people to raise their hand if they’d been abducted by aliens. I scroll down to see if anyone has something to say on the comments below, but it’s all just speculation; looks like into_thin_air wasn’t lucky enough to have a participant in his audience. Or maybe the “government” was “censoring” them.

I started searching around a bunch of other websites I remembered Kirk mentioning over the years. BigfootsBestFriend.com, CosmoWatcher.org… none of them seemed to know about the Magnolia Project. Into_thin_air put out a line, and no one would take it, because it read like it was from their own imagination.

I was onto something now. So into it, in fact, that I found myself dialing Kirk’s phone number. He picked up.

“Hey, remember when you mentioned that you were going to work for BlackoutBunker?” I asked him, once we’d bypassed the pleasantries, “Did you ever meet the head of the website?”

Kirk sounded a little defensive. “I mean, we exchanged emails. It’s not like we got coffee. They could be anywhere in the world, for all I know. A lot of this business operates in secrecy for a reason, you know. One day you might leak something really big and a powerful enemy might get interested. They already take a pretty big risk putting this stuff on the net.”

Of course Kirk had talked a big game. If they’d met in person. I just wanted to know what kind of person would write this stuff online. Were they some bored housewife in Kentucky, a paranoid college student in Florida, a withering old man who never left his house? Did this person actually have a connection to my father, or was it all made up?

“Do the words Magnolia Project mean anything to you?” I asked Kirk.

“Nope. What is it?”

“It’s… nothing,” I said, “Don’t worry about it.”

I stopped myself for a moment. Why was I doing this? Was I really going to search out a completely flimsy source that couldn’t be credited, simply because I didn’t have anything better to do? Odds were, this person on the Internet was inventing some fiction about psychics, and they picked a cool-sounding name for it—a name that, coincidentally, overlapped with my father’s work. I mean, Michael didn’t strike me as a truther, but he still knew about the words Magnolia Project when no one else did.

“Hey, what’s all this about, anyway? You haven’t talked to me in a week and now you’re prowling the dark web?” Kirk said, “I thought you hated this stuff.”

“I’ve… learned to address my biases.”

For a moment, I thought about it. Why didn’t I loop Kirk in at the beginning? Sure, he greatly underestimated the amount of peril my father was in, and he never seemed to take anything seriously. But we were leaving the realm of serious. I could mend my friendship with him if I would just be honest, but… that was it. Were we really friends? Shouldn’t it have been him, and not Michael, there with me in the library four days ago?

“You know, Mr. Miller won’t let you cite this stuff on papers. I got in trouble last time.”

“It’s not for school. It’s a favor, for a friend,” I lied, grasping desperately for something generic.

“A friend? Who?” Kirk’s tone changed. Oh no. I said the wrong thing. That was the thing, Kirk and me. We never had other friends. Definitely not ones that were into this kind of stuff. “Was it that Mitchell kid? Your lab partner?”

“No!” I said, “His name is Michael, and—no, not him. I gotta go, Kirk. Talk to you later.”

I quickly hung up and tossed the phone away from me as if it was a live wire.

I promised Michael I wouldn’t talk about it. And if I knew anything Kirk, it was that he couldn’t keep his damn mouth shut.


Michael had a plan since he was young, should he ever need it, to escape his mother’s watchful eye. He wanted to save it for a really good occasion, dire emergencies only. Because honestly, he was terrified that he would get caught, or it wouldn’t work. But today, he decided he knew at last what that dire emergency was.

The first part of his plan: request spaghetti for dinner. This was crucial because spaghetti was delicious, and it would fill him up and give him the courage he needed to carry out the rest of his plan. It was also important because his mother ate hers with grated Parmesan cheese on top. This led to the second part of the plan: pour crushed up sleeping pills onto the top of her spaghetti.

Michael got the over-the-counter kind, NOT the prescription kind—although they might be weaker, he didn’t want to kill her. He just wanted a few hours free from her watchful eye. When he crushed them up with a mortar and pestle, they looked just like Parmesan cheese. Mixed in with the actual cheese flakes, she would never be able to tell the difference. As his mother was rounding up the dishes near the sink to be washed, and the spaghetti sat on the table ready-to-eat, he sprinkled the elicit substance right on top of hers. It was almost too easy.

They ate together at the small kitchen table, watching the sun setting outside of the window. The boxwoods hid it from view right before it was its most beautiful, so sunsets at the McKenna household were short and dull. Michael was too squeamish to really pay attention anyway. He watched as she twirled up the spaghetti with her fork and spoon, then carefully ate every last bite.

“How was your day at school?” she asked.

He couldn’t chicken out now. He had to follow through. “It was fun. I learned a lot. I actually had a bunch of tests, and they really took it out of me.”

“Really?”

“Yeah, in fact—” he faked a yawn, “—I think I might turn in early.”

“Early? Well, that’s certainly new,” she admitted, “I thought you liked staying up late for the eleven o’ clock news.”

“Yeah, well, all the death, despair, and danger is going to have to wait. I’ll catch it tomorrow,” He developed a habit of watching the eleven o’clock news a long time ago. That’s when they saved all the grisly stuff, for the parents to watch after their kids had gone to bed. Michael liked it because it showed him all the dangerous, terrible things going on outside of his four walls.

His mother starts to yawn too, probably catching his yawn. “I guess my day was pretty long, too. I wouldn’t mind going to bed earlier than usual. It’s a good habit, anyway.”

“It’s a fantastic habit,” he advised her.

Despite the pills, he still had to wait a whole hour before his mother seemed positively groggy. It took everything he had—he put on easy listening music and turned up the thermostat three degrees. At last, she finally couldn’t stop yawning, and sat up from the couch.

“I’m going upstairs,” she said, putting her bookmark in her book, “You going to be alright?”

“I always am,” he said, and watched her go, trying to contain his giddiness.

For the final part of the plan, he listened closely to the baby monitor he hid under his mother’s bed until he was absolutely sure she was fast asleep. When her steady breathing came through for a solid five minutes with no disturbance, he threw on his coat and shoes. His heart started to race. This was it. This was the danger he craved! He was going to escape at last! He was going to go on a date—with a girl!
