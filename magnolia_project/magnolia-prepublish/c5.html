Chapter 5

My house was messy. Even on the best of days, it looks like a tornado has hit it, with all of our belongings scattered haphazardly on the floor. No one in our family got my dad's cleanliness gene.

"Just kick your shoes off to the left, and hang your coat up here," I take my arms out of the sleeves of my wool coat and hang it on the rack, already full to bursting with coats, hats, and gloves.

"What happened to your house?" Michael wonders aloud, which is a bit rude of him, but I respond anyway.

"I have two brothers. They’re twins," I tell him, stepping around him towards the kitchen, which connects to the dining room through the back, "I don't recommend it."

He unlaced his boots and set them neatly next to each other. We walked towards the foyer, and I stopped him. "Careful!" Michael looks down and sees that he was moments away from stepping on some pointy, spiky toy of Trevor's.

"Thanks," he carefully steps around it and watches his feet as we head into the kitchen and then the dining room.

"Mom! We're back!" I yelled through the house, and she comes out of the dining room with a glass of white wine in her hand.

"Good, please, help me set the table," she sighed, and then looks at Michael and held out her hand to shake his. "Hello, you must be Grace's friend. I didn't get your name."

"Michael," he answered. Mom was still in her work clothes, a pressed blouse and a blue skirt, hair in a bun. She lingered for a moment in the handshake, as if analyzing his face for something I couldn't see, but then she let go.

"Nice to meet you. Just come on in and have a seat." She waved us in. Michael washes his hands in the sink. I take out a stack of plates and set them up all around the table, six places. My brothers come thundering down the stairs the moment they hear the sound of the silverware clinking together, but instead of fighting over seats as usual, they sneak quietly into the kitchen and take seats like civilized people. Elsie’s rein has already started to take over this house.

“Michael, meet Max and Trevor, my brothers,” I said.

“They seem nice,” he said, watching them sitting at the table calmly, “I wish I had siblings.”

“No you don’t. That’s an illusion. Watch out for ketchup packets underneath the toilet seat,” I advised him.

Michael thought about this. "Maybe not, then."

Elsie swept in from the kitchen with a large dish of macaroni in hand. "Dinner is served, everyone," Max and Trevor immediately lunge at the plate with their spoons, but a sharp look from Elsie and they quickly retreated. I started off the circle, snatched a large helping, and pass the plate to Michael. As the food goes around and I wait to eat, I realize Elsie is staring at Michael with rapt attention. Maybe trying to find a way to shred his self-confidence. Michael is too normal though. He doesn’t wear his hair long, or any outlandish clothes. When everyone is served, there are a few minutes of content silence punctuated with the sounds of eating from my brothers. Elsie keeps looking at Michael suspiciously, but my theory was right so far: bringing him did break up the tension. Eventually, my mother speaks up.

"So, what's this science project on?" She asked, and I swallowed.

Michael answered before I could. “Fire.”

I shook my head. Bad subject. He caught on only a little too late. “Uh, I mean, like the flame test. You know, where you can tell what kind of substance something is by the color it burns?”

This answer seemed to placate my family. But Elsie was still intent on staring at him.

"So, Michael, you're a senior; are you looking to study science in college?" She asked. College again. Every conversation with her is headed in one way. It’s not like the University down the road isn’t constantly reminding us even when she’s not here.

"I don't think so," he said. “This food is truly amazing, Ms.—”

“Please, call me Aunt Elsie,” she says, “What do you think you’ll study then? Politics? The law?”

“Oh, no, I’m not going to college,” he didn’t even blink an eye, “Not… really my thing.”

I felt like choking on my food. Maybe if I did, it would create enough of a distraction. Someone not pursuing higher education? In my Aunt Elsie’s eyes, that was the path of the garbage collector, the mailman, the fast food worker.

“Come on, smart boy like you?” Aunt Elsie kept her voice deceptively sweet, “It’s a good way to support a family.”

“Yeah, I’m not so sure about the whole family thing, either. Not many families in my experience have… inspired much confidence in me,” he said. Michael quickly polished off his food and pushed his plate to the side. "Where is your bathroom?" He asked my mother.

"Up the stairs, to the left. Holler if you need anything," she said, and Michael stood up, depositing the napkin on his plate. The rest of us continued eating.

I focused on my food, but Elsie gave me this weird look, as if she knew something I didn’t.

“I hate to break this to you, love,” she said, although she sounded positively excited to say it, “But I think your boyfriend is… you know…” she looked to my brothers, as if she’s afraid saying the word ‘gay’ would suddenly turn their young, impressionable minds.

I opened my mouth to say “he’s not gay”, but instead it came out “he’s not my boyfriend.”

Minutes passed, and Michael did not return. When dinner finishes, I scooped up his plate underneath mine and carried them both to the sink. He was still missing. Looking for him, I marched up the stairs just as he came out of a door at the end of the hallway.

"That's not the bathroom," I said.

"Yeah, I figured that out," he replied.

"What were you doing in there? That’s my dad’s study,” I looked over his shoulder to see what was inside, but Michael quickly closed the door behind him so I can’t see inside.

“I really gotta get going, I can’t believe how late it’s gotten,” he pushed past me to start down the stairs, two at a time. He tripped over the runner but kept on plowing forwards. I basically had to run to keep up with him.

“Michael? What’s the rush?” my mother asked, watching him stumble towards the door.

“I really have to go. Completely lost track of time. The food was delicious, though. See you later, maybe,” he said without even stopping.

"If you’re worried about biking back in the dark, we can just take you back in the car,” she assured him, coming out of the kitchen still holding a dishrag and a plate. "You haven’t had any time to work on your project. I'd hate for your time to get cut short."

"Really, Mrs. Hall, we'll be fine. We have time to work on it in class tomorrow as well," he started to put his boots on the wrong feet and didn’t realize until he stood up. She looked to me, but I just shrugged. I had no idea why he’s acting so strange. Did it have to do with something he saw in my father’s study?

Finally, my mom gave up. "Alright. It was nice meeting you, Michael."

"You too, Mrs. Hall," I was chasing him out the door as he bids her pleasantries.

"I’ll see him out," I volunteered, and then opened the door, walking out behind him in my bare feet. The sky was fallen completely dark and street lamps dot the street with their yellow glow.

“Michael? Where are you going? I thought we were going to look into my dad’s arrest,” I said.

“That’s okay, maybe another time. Or maybe never. I’m starting to wonder if this is really worth our efforts,” he pulled his bike up to its wheels, buckled his helmet.

“Not worth our efforts? But Michael, my father—”

“Bye, Grace!” He kicked off from the ground and whips around the corner, skidding on the pavement as he turned.
